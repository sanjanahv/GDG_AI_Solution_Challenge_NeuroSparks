1.  Executive Summary
Financial Triage Environment is an Indian-household-finance simulation built on the OpenEnv framework. The agent — an LLM or any policy — manages a rupee-denominated household ledger for 30 to 90 simulated days. Each step represents one day: the agent decides what to pay, what to defer, which loan to refuse, and whether to build or drain its savings. The environment returns a dense per-step scalar reward and a single deterministic grade in [0, 1] at the episode's close.
The project was developed for the Meta OpenEnv × Scaler School of Technology hackathon. It targets two competition tracks: long-horizon planning (30/60/90-day interest-and-bill compounding) and economic/world modelling (stochastic paydays, partial-observability between loan labels and APRs, mechanical overdraft and default).
All amounts are denominated in Indian Rupees (₹ / INR). APRs, Diwali magnitudes, informal-loan rates, and UPI spend distributions are calibrated against published Indian-finance statistics cited in tasks.py — not fit to any real household panel.
 
2.  Core Concept & Motivation
2.1  The Problem
Most Indians learn household finance by getting it wrong: a missed EMI, a recharge late enough to disconnect a SIM, a Diwali loan that looked small on the offer screen. Traditional financial-literacy tools use static charts and do not let a learner (or a language model) feel the compounding consequences of daily decisions.
2.2  The Solution
Financial Triage makes an LLM play the month — feel paydays, interest bleed, UPI micro-spend, and the 1 a.m. instant-cash pitch — and then judges how the books look at the end. State transitions are deterministic Python code (server/my_env_environment.py), not prompt-based vibes. The grader (tasks.py) is rule-based and reproducible: the same seed always produces the same result.
2.3  OpenEnv Fit
The submission conforms to the OpenEnv HTTP contract: POST /reset, POST /step, GET /state, GET /metadata, GET /schema, GET /health, and GET /mcp are all live on the Space. AutoEnv.from_hub() works with OPENENV_TRUST_REMOTE_CODE=1.

4.  Environment Design
4.1  The Daily Loop
Every step is one simulated day. The OpenEnv lifecycle is:
reset(task_id) — returns the opening ledger (observation dict)
step(action) — advances the clock by one day: paydays land, UPI spends fire, interest accrues, bills come due, shocks roll their dice, reward breakdown is computed, next observation is returned
grade_episode — when the horizon closes, the grader reads the full history and returns a single float in [0, 1]
 
4.2  Observation
The observation returned after each step is a structured ledger containing:
Account balances: checking, savings
Active bills with due dates and amounts
Outstanding debts with APR, remaining principal, and next EMI
Risk signals: days to overdraft, interest accruing today, credit score, late-fee risk
Optional loan offers (formal, informal, festive)
Active medical emergency details (if any)
Festival metadata (if Diwali window is active)
Short human-readable daily_summary string
The informal-lender quote in the observation advertises a per-day rate that hides a 240–365% APR. The agent can see the actual APR if it looks.

Average household debt service ratio: 25.7% of disposable income
 
6.  Stochasticity & Reproducibility
All random elements are seeded, so every number in the README is reproducible on a laptop. Key stochastic mechanisms:
Bill amounts jitter ± 15% around their base value each cycle
UPI micro-spend fires with a per-day probability and draws from a configured amount range
Medical emergency timing is drawn from the task config's shock schedule
Informal-lender APR is partially observable — the daily-rate label in the observation undersells the annualised figure
The stochasticity audit script (scripts/verify_stochasticity.py) shows ₹3,288 std in day-10 checking balance on medium across 30 seeds, while σ(score) = 0 — the state evolves stochastically but the event-driven grader maps to the same grade at this configuration. For seed-level comparison the dense per-step return (which GRPO optimises) has real variance. Full results are committed as stochasticity_report.json.
 
7.  Training Pipeline
7.1  Stage 1 — Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
SFT is behavioural cloning on heuristic rollouts stored in sft_dataset.jsonl (629 kB, ~750 episodes across all three difficulties). The objective is not to teach a good policy but to teach the action grammar and a sane opening repertoire so the GRPO parser never needs to fail-safe.
Base model: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (4-bit Unsloth quantisation)
Training: 60 steps; log-loss drops from 2.25 to 0.12 on the heuristic-rollout dataset
Checkpoints: LoRA adapters saved locally; merge with Unsloth for single-file export
SFT result: recovers 86% (medium) to 95% (hard) of heuristic episode grade from text alone
 
7.2  Stage 2 — GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimisation)
GRPO runs in TRL on the Unsloth-quantised Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct model. The reward signal is the live environment, not a separate judge model.
Per-row mechanics:
Each GRPO row carries a stored prefix of expert (heuristic) actions for a specific (task_id, seed, day) tuple
The trainer deterministically replays that prefix to reconstruct the exact day-d observation
The model emits one action string; the parser strictly accepts or rejects it (no heuristic fallback)
One env.step() runs; the optimizer sees _last_breakdown['total'] as the scalar reward
The end-of-episode bonus is folded into the last-day reward so the model sees the grader's tail
grade_episode is not called inside GRPO — it is reserved for the bar-chart metric and the Space's /score endpoint, keeping the two signals distinct. Reward-invariant unit tests in tests/test_reward_properties.py check: on-time beats do-nothing, overdraft fires at −25, do-nothing streak triggers the inaction term, same-day withdraw-redeposit earns no savings credit, grade ∈ (0,1) on every difficulty.
 
7.3  Training Results (7B Model, Single-Pass n = 5)
 
Full hyperparameters and per-step scalar metrics are preserved in TRAINING_LOGS/training_run.json. Both training scripts set report_to='wandb' when WANDB_API_KEY is present, so re-runners get the full per-step W&B log without the author's key in the loop.
 
8.  Multi-Seed Baselines (n = 60 Seeds)
Produced by scripts/eval_heuristic.py with n = 60 seeds per (policy, task) and a percentile bootstrap on the mean. Raw per-seed scores are in heuristic_scores.json.
 
Key findings: Easy is near-solved by the heuristic. Medium shows a clean ordering heuristic ≫ random ≫ do_nothing. On Hard, the heuristic only edges random_valid by Δ ≈ 0.002 at n = 60 — this is intentional. It keeps the environment genuinely hard for the SFT → GRPO loop and gives the model real room to beat the teacher. greedy_apr (the conventional personal-finance advice) drops below do_nothing on medium and hard because paying the highest-APR debt first drains checking ahead of bills and medical shocks.
 
9.  Environment Ablation: The Mechanics Actually Bind
Same heuristic, same n = 40 seeds, one configured mechanic nulled out per run on the hard task. Positive Δ means the mechanic is binding — the score rises when it is removed. Bars are sorted by effect size with 95% paired bootstrap CI over matched seeds.
 
On medium, only one mechanic binds: no_interest_accrual lifts the grade from 0.694 → 0.818 (+0.124). The other four ablations leave medium grade unchanged within 0.01. This is the mechanical answer to 'why is the rule-based teacher capped on medium?' — the APR stack is doing the work, not the grader.

13.  Hackathon Rubric Map
Track fit: #2 Long-horizon planning (30/60/90-day arcs) and #3.1 Economic/world modelling (stochastic paydays, partial observability, mechanical default and overdraft). Multi-agent (#1) and self-spawning curricula (#4) are explicitly out of scope.
 
14.  Honest Caveats
On hard, the heuristic only edges random_valid by Δ ≈ 0.002 at n = 60. The rule-based teacher is not optimal — this is a feature, not a bug. The per-seed scatter in paired_scores_hard.png shows the win/loss split directly rather than hiding it in the mean.
On easy and medium, rounded grades flatten across seeds. The env is not deterministic — the stochasticity audit confirms state evolves (σ(checking) = ₹3,288 on medium at day 10) but the event-driven grader maps to the same grade at this configuration.
APRs, Diwali magnitudes, and UPI leak rates are calibrated from published Indian-finance statistics cited in tasks.py. They are not fit to any real household panel.
The grader is a design object, not an econometric estimate.
The environment is a decision-making study. It does not hand out financial advice and should not be treated as a product.
Planned next steps: seed-matched model-minus-heuristic plot once new GRPO checkpoints land; sampled (rather than hard-coded) emergency timing and informal-loan APRs; LoRA adapters pushed as a separate Model Hub repo.
